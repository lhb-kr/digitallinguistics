Detailed Guide

The PBL (Problem-Based Learning) module runs for four weeks. During the first three weeks, your team works together to solve the assigned problem. In the fourth week, there is no class, but you will read and evaluate other teams' reports.

The Week 4 evaluation counts as attendance and contributes to your score, so it is mandatory.

Final Report

Your deliverable is an interactive web page uploaded to GitHub. Submit the link through the designated channel. The instructor will explain the technical details in Week 2 or 3.

⏰ Report deadline: Week 3, the 16th, at 2:00 PM.

The PBL problem has been uploaded to the LMS. It may not seem overly complex, but the multi-step process is designed to give you enough time to discuss thoroughly and produce a polished result.

Mandatory Check-In

You must come to the assigned classroom and check in at the start of every session. If you skip the check-in, any work done elsewhere will not be recognized and will be recorded as an absence.

Flexible End Time

After checking in, your team can decide when to wrap up. You do not need to use the full class time. If your problem-solving has progressed well, you can finish early. However, you must submit the progress report and notify the instructor before leaving.

Doubled Penalties

⚠ Absences during the PBL module cause serious harm to your team. Attendance point deductions are doubled during this period.

Location Flexibility

After checking in, your team may move to another room or any other location (e.g., a cafe). However, you must either inform the instructor of your location or arrange a Zoom meeting so the required consultation can still happen.

What Is the IFLA Chart?

A structured brainstorming and planning framework with four sections:

  1. Ideas: Brainstorm everything related to the problem. All ideas welcome.
  2. Facts: Things from the brainstorm that you already know and do not need to research further.
  3. Learning Issues: Things you identified that you do not know and need to study or research.
  4. Action: The concrete plan for how you will address the learning issues and move forward.

When and How to Submit

  • Day 1 (first session): Complete all four sections (I, F, L, A).
  • Day 2 onward: Only the Action plan section is required.
  • Write your chart on Padlet and submit the link. The instructor will review it and give feedback.
⚠ Important: Do not begin other activities until the IFLA chart is complete. Finish it first, then start your work.

Purpose

Submitted every session. The instructor uses it to track progress, confirm individual contributions, and provide feedback. Incorporate that feedback into your continued work.

Components

Team Summary (one per team): A brief, free-format summary of what the team planned, accomplished, and intends to do next. If you want specific feedback on something, emphasize it here.

Individual Materials (one per person): Each member submits their own work. No required format. Post a summary, upload raw materials, attach files, or anything that shows what you have been working on.

Format Expectations

Do not spend time polishing the report's appearance. The priority is clarity: the instructor needs to see what progress was made and what each person contributed.

⚠ Failing to post your progress report on Slack will be treated as if you did not do the work for that day.

All progress reports are submitted through Slack. Here is what you need to do:

  1. Create a Slack account (if you do not already have one).
  2. The team leader creates a workspace named with your team name.
  3. Create a channel called "Progress Report."
  4. Invite all team members to the workspace.
  5. Invite the instructor using: j-lee@korea.ac.kr

You may also use Slack as a general collaboration tool. How you use it beyond the Progress Report channel is up to you.

💡 Slack is a collaboration tool used by many companies. Getting experience with it now is part of the learning goals of this course.

At the end of each week (after the Wednesday session), every team member must complete a self and peer evaluation. Results are partially reflected in your PBL module activity score.

Your team's ground rules (set on Day 1) serve as the evaluation criteria.

🚨 If you do not complete the evaluation by the deadline, your peer evaluation score is automatically 0. Complete it right after Wednesday's class. Do not forget.

The first session includes several one-time setup tasks in addition to the regular workflow.

Self-Introductions

Get to know your teammates. No specific prompts. Just do it naturally.

Choose a Team Leader

The team leader has four assigned responsibilities:

🗣 Run meetings smoothly
📞 Collect contact info
📋 Confirm summary submitted
🕑 Decide daily end time

Additional responsibilities can be assigned by the team. The team leader receives bonus points in the instructor's PBL activity evaluation.

Set a Team Name

Choose a creative name. This will also be used for your Slack workspace.

Establish Ground Rules (3-5)

Create ground rules within your team. These will later be used as evaluation criteria for self and peer evaluations. Post them on Slack.

Set Up Slack

Complete the full setup as described in Section 5. The team leader takes the lead.

The final deliverable is not a traditional written report. You will create an interactive web page based on your findings and upload it to GitHub. Submit the GitHub link as your final submission.

Think back to the first class when the instructor introduced the PBL module with a web page and a quiz. Your report should follow a similar format.

Detailed instructions on how to build and upload will be provided in Week 2 or 3.
